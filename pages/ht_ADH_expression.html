Expression in E. Coli


  1. Transform BL21(DE3) cells, plate, grow a culture and have plasmid sequenced to be sure everything is correct.

  2. Store BL21(DE3) cells in 10% glycerol at -80.

  3. Grow a 6 mL stock solution of BL21(DE3) from the frozen glycerol stock for 4 hours (until you see some turbidity) at 37oC.

  4. Add 1 mL of the stock to each of 6 4L flasks containing 1.6 L of LB medium + 30 mg/L Kanamycin + .25 mM ZnSO4 (add both using a sterile filter AFTER autoclaving the media) and grow at 37oC for 6 -8 hours with shaking at 250rpm.

  5. Induce expression by adding IPTG to a final concentration of .5 mM.

  6. After 12-16 hours, centrifuge cells at 6,000 rpm for 10 minutes, repeat until the entire culture is in pellet form.

  7. Resuspend in 50 mM Tris + 5 mM EDTA, collect in three centrifuge bottles and centrifuge for 15 minutes at 10,000 rpm (use water as a balance for each one).

  8. Transfer cell paste to three separate containers for storage at -80. I use cellophane – just don’t let too many wrinkles form, and it will be easy to peel out the cells when you thaw them later. Record the mass of the cellophane alone, and then after adding the cells to determine the yield of the cell culture.





Protocol for purification of htADH from E. Coli


 before you start, have a look at the protein purification table, so you don’t forget to measure anything important.


  1. Thaw cell paste on bench top (about 1 hr) in a steel beaker. Use 10 to 15 grams of cell paste, which represents the yield from 3L of cell culture. This purification can be carried out at room temperature, except where indicated in the protocol. Move fast anyway to limit the action of uninhibited proteases and protein denaturation.


  1. Add 100 mL Lysis Buffer: 50 mM Tris, pH 8.0; 1 mM DTT; 1 mM PMSF (protease inhibitor); 2 mM EDTA (chelates free metals, keeps metalloprotease activity down). DTT and PMSF should be prepared the day of the purification because they deteriorate in solution. Make PMSF in 250 L of methanol. The other ingredients can be added from stock solutions. Mix until the cell paste is thoroughly resuspended.



  1. Add lysozyme to a final concentration of 0.5 mg/mL. (dissolve it in buffer first, then add in by stiring).


  1. Incubate for about 1 hour. When lysis is complete, the mixture will be very viscous and sticky because of DNA. In the last 15 minutes of this step, transfer the solution to an ice bucket.


  1. Sonicate for 10 minutes (on ice!) to break up the DNA. Add some water to the ice bucket, and make sure the bottom of the steel beaker is touching the bottom of the bucket. Otherwise it will sink, and the tip of the sonicator will froth the sample. The tip should be submerged by at least ½ inch. Check for remaining viscocity by mixing, and sonicate for an additional 3-5 minutes if necessary. On our sonicator, the settings are level 6 (of 10) for the power output, and 30% duty cycle, which means 0.3 seconds running and 0.7 seconds not running. In the past, we had used DNase instead of sonicating; we only stopped because this way is cheaper, and doesn’t appear to damage the enzyme.


  1. Transfer to a large centrifuge bottle and centrifuge for 30 minutes at 10,000 rpm. Prepare a water bath at 60oC.


  1. Measure the specific activity using the standard assay for htADH: 20 oC, 10 mM H-Benzyl Alcohol, and 2.5 mM NAD+. Note the total volume, and Save 50 L and freeze for a Bradford assay and gel to be done at the end of the purification. This is Aliquot #1.


  1. Transfer the supernatant from step 6 to a fresh centrifuge bottle, and place this in the 60oC bath for 15 minutes. Lots of junk should crash out; the solution should be milky. This step denatures most proteins, but the thermophilic ADH will be fine. It’s into that.


  1. Add protamine sulfate (a small cationic protein that binds and precipitates DNA) to a final concentration of 1 mg/mL. This will need to be dissolved in around 10 mL of lysis buffer about 30 minutes in advance.


  1. Measure the specific activity of the supernatant as above, and save aliquot #2.


  1. Centrifuge for 15 minutes at 10,000 rpm.


  1. ** I have found you can skip these steps and load directly to the DEAE column, but I am leaving it in this write up in case you want to reproduce older methods ** Make a 4L (cold) solution of 25 mM Sodium Phosphate (aka “NaPi”), pH 7.5, + 0.25 mM DTT, and soak two 12 inch lengths of dialysis tubing. We use 10,000 MWCO (molecular weight cut off) Snakeskin Tubing from Pierce. From here onward, everything is done in the cold room (4oC). After the tubing has soaked for 5 minutes, clamp one end, keep a second clamp handy to seal it, and get a funnel to transfer the protein solution to the tubing.


  1. Transfer the supernatant from Step 10 to dialysis tubing, clamp it shut, and allow to soak 4 hours. The buffer solution should be stirring, with a 10 mL plastic pipet dropped in the beaker to keep the dialysis tubing from going round and round and round with the swirling buffer. Since the protein is stable, you can to do the dialysis overnight. But the fastest way to do this prep is to continue all the way to the first column, doing the wash step overnight (step 12).


  1. If precipitate has collected in the dialysis tubing, centrifuge for 10 minutes at 10,000 rpm. This hasn’t been a problem recently (the purification has been optimized somewhat) so make a note if it does occur.


  1. Note the volume(it may have changed), and measure the specific activity as above, saving as aliquot #3.


  1. Load the solution onto a DEAE column equilibrated with dialysis buffer, and then wash the column with the same buffer (no pump; gravity gives a good flow rate). My column is about 12 inches tall and 1 inch in diameter. Monitor the eluate at this point (using UV monitor); a lot of junk will come off, but the protein, as well as some pink and yellow impurities, will stay near the top of the column. When the absorbance goes back down you can elute the protein. 2-3 hours seems to do it. You could do this if you were in a hurry, but it is better to wash overnight with the current and recommended protocol, because more of the colored (and other) impurities are removed this way.


  1. To elute the protein, connect the column to a fraction collector. Run a salt gradient: The starting solution is just the equilibration buffer, and the final solution is the same buffer with 250 mM NaCl added. The total volume of the gradient doesn’t need to be more than 300 mL, but you may want to prepare extra 250 mM (NaCl) buffer in case some of the protein is still eluting at the end of the gradient (happens sometimes). This takes about 2 hours.


  1. You could run a gel at this point to see which fractions are purest, but my experience has been that the resolution is not very good, and the best thing is just to take the most active fractions and then concentrate the protein. The “most active” fractions are those that have at least 10% of the activity found in the most active fraction.


  1. After you have pooled the fractions, note their total volume, and Measure the specific activity as above, saving as aliquot #4.


  1. To concentrate the protein, we use an Amicon stirring cell with a 50,000 MWCO Polyethersulfone filter (cellulose is OK too) under 50 psi of Nitrogen gas. This can take several hours, but should not be done overnight – you need to monitor closely toward the end!


  1. In order to lower the ionic strength, once the protein is down to 10 mL or less, I add 25 mM NaPi up to around 50 mL. This may require an additional 2-3 hours of protein concentration. Be careful, because the time it takes depends on how much protein you have, and this will vary from one preparation to the next. Ultimately, you want to concentrate the protein as much as possible before the next column – around 1-2 mL, but pay close attention near the end of the concentration step. If you over-concentrate, this will hurt your yield considerably.



  1. We next load the protein onto an affinity column specific to enzymes that bind nucleotide cofactors. The column is 5’-AMP Sepharose 4B from Amersham Biosciences. This column needs to be equilibrated with the fresh buffer (identical to that used in dialysis) on the day when you intend to use it. Before loading the protein, remove the layer of buffer down to the bed of the column until it is exposed. Make sure the valve at the bottom of the column is closed when you do this, or it will run dry. Once the protein is concentrated down to 1-2 mL, load it directly onto the exposed bed of the affinity column using a pipet, and allow it to bind by gravity (stay in the room, this happens fast). As soon as the protein has completely entered the column resin, add a small amount (~500 L) of buffer, and allow this to fully enter the resin as well, before filling up the reservoir with bufferThe column should be equilibrated with the same buffer as has been used throughout; the dialysis buffer.



  1. Hook up the column to a fraction collector immediately. Because psADH has a mM KD for NAD+, it does not require elution using a salt or nucleotide gradient; it simply has a longer retention time than most of the impurities when you wash with the equilibration buffer. Collect the fractions that elute later, beginning with those that do not contain any discernable color. You can check fractions by running a gel if yield is a concern; otherwise toss fractions when in doubt. We still get a good yield (greater than 50 mg from 3 L of E. coli). Before concentrating, Note the total volume, and measure the specific activity of the supernatant as above, and save aliquot #5.


  1. Concentrate down as much as you want, snap freeze in liquid N2 and store at -80. We stored ADH at -20oC in glycerol in the past, but I have found that the protein loses activity after a month or so, and that storing at -80oC with no addition keeps ~100% activity. Multiple freez-thaw cycles seem to be ok for the thermophile.



  1. Fill out the purification table after taking appropriate assays, and run a gel on the fractions you saved, taking 50 g in one lane, and 10 g in a second lane as a means of judging purity. You will want to carry out the Bradford assay before you run the gel in order to know how much total protein you are loading. For the Bradford, a rough estimate is that you will need to dilute samples #1 through #3 about 50 fold, and take 10-20 L for the Bradford Assay. Sample #4 will need to be diluted about 10 fold, and the final pooled fractions from the affinity column, #5, will not need to be diluted at all.
